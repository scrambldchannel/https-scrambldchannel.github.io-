Analysising Open Data from the Edinburgh Cycle Scheme

Posted on Tue 08 September 2020 in python

I noticed recently that the Edinburgh Cycle Hire scheme makes the data collected available for app creators or anyone wanting to do do some analysis. With cycling said to have exploded during the Covid lockdown I thought it would be interesting to take a look to see if this anecdotal evidence was reflected in the data. Of course the journeys made by these bikes only reflect a small subset of cycling but one I suspect is skewed toward new or lapsed riders. Of course the data doesn't you anything about the rider, or much at all really. It only contains the date, the start and end points and the duration but this should be enough to identify some trends.

Marathons or Sprints?¶

It isn't possible to get a true measure of the distance bikes are ridden but it is possible to measure the distance between the start and end points of the journey. Given the short average duration of rides, it follows that most journeys will be on the short side but I was curious as to whether anyone had ridden all the way from Queensferry to Queen Margaret University. For this I used geopy but not before going down the rabbit hole of just how complicated calculating the distance between two points can be.

Cycling is trending!¶

The scheme has been in operation since September 2018 which, on reflection, seems a strange month to launch such a scheme. Overall the number of trips has been increasing steadily for the same month in subsequent years with a big jump as the Covid lockdown came into force in April. The combination of more free time, quieter roads and the onset of summer led to a big increase. This mirrors anecdotal reports of increased cycling levels generally. What I really like about this chart though is that it does look a little bit like the Loch Ness monster.

Commuters or weekend warriors?¶

I suspected that the bikes might get more use on weekends which turns out to be somewhat true but not hugely significant. Looking at the current data, usage does seem more skewed toward weekends for 2020 but given that 2019 is the only year we have data for every month, it's not really enough to draw any conclusions.

Night owls or early birds?¶

Looking at when the bikes are hired shows a clear difference between weekday and weekend use. On Weekends, hires peaks in the early afternoon with a normalish distribution either side. Weekdays show a clear uptick in the hours between 7 and 9am and while rides do rise during the middle of the day, they continue to increase showing a peak between the hours of 4 and 6pm. This seems to suggest that they are being used by people commuting to and from school or work, particularly on the way home. This assumes the majority of people follow a traditional 9-5 pattern on weekdays.

Where are people going?¶

This is perhaps the most interesting question for me. The locations of the stations have changed over time as the scheme has been tinkered with and stations have been moved to accomodate works so this might not tell the full story, but should give a flavour of how the bikes are used. It turns out that the most popular individual journeys are various round trips with Crammond and Porty being the most popular.

More interesting is looking at which A to B journeys are the most common. I would not have guessed that Canonmills to Victoria Quay would be the most popular but a lot of people work there, it's close to the bars and restaurants of The Shore and has a great cycle segregated cycle path most of the way. It's not the easiest trip to make by bus either which is also true of Duke Street (near Leith Links) to Portobello and back. Ever since the number 12 bus route was truncated, this is a really frustrating journey by public transport and the bikes would offer a good solution.

Conclusions?¶

It's clear that the cycle scheme has experienced a high amount of growth during lockdown, which coincided with a pretty good summer. Whether the rates of use continue to grow or taper off is difficult so say but there's an argument that once you've got used to using them, and paid your annual subscription, you might be more likely to keep using them. You might also be likely to buy yourself a bike if you're really enjoying it. It will be intresting to see how the scheme develops but it's certainly a sign that there is an appetite for cycling in Edinburgh.
